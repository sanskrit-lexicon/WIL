## When to use WIL

Use WIL for **historical comparison** with MW — to see the sense-divisions that MW inherited from the 1832 baseline. WIL has no source-citation apparatus (0.005 ls/rec) so is not useful for citation tracing. Its systematic grammatical tagging (99.8% gram block) is a strength for morphological lookup.

---

## Relationship to MW

WIL is MW's direct English-language ancestor. Monier-Williams used the 1832 WIL as the base for both the [1872 first edition](https://www.sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/MW72Scan/2020/web/index.php) and the [1899 second edition](https://www.sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/MWScan/2020/web/index.php), supplementing it with PWG's philological apparatus. CDSL classifies WIL as a `koSa` (kosha), reflecting its origin at [Fort William College, Calcutta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fort_William_College) with Indian pandits working from classical kosha sense-divisions.
